Conclusion

The EU battery passport represents a paradigm shift in how batteries are designed, manufactured, used and recycled.  It forces supply chains to become transparent, traceable and sustainable.  Major Fortune 500 companies—from EV pioneers like BMW, Volkswagen and Volvo to battery makers like CATL, LG Energy Solution and Samsung SDI—are already piloting passports through GBA and Battery Pass initiatives  .  Consumer‑electronics giants, industrial‑equipment manufacturers and raw‑material suppliers will also need to comply.

Existing enterprise systems (ERP, PLM, MES, BMS, traceability platforms) provide a foundation, but additional components—unique identifiers, distributed ledgers, standardised data models, AI‑driven analytics and open APIs—are essential for compliance.  By adopting a federated, AI‑first platform, companies can not only meet regulatory requirements but also gain competitive advantages through improved supply‑chain resilience, predictive maintenance, reduced environmental footprint and new business models (second‑life markets, recycling partnerships).  The design outlined above aims to balance transparency with data sovereignty and to leverage AI to ensure that every battery’s story—from mine to recycling—is trustworthy, auditable and optimised for a sustainable future.
